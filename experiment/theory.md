The Brookfield viscometer measures viscosity based on the torque required to rotate a spindle immersed in the sample.
When the spindle rotates in a viscous liquid or semisolid, the sample resists the motion. This resistance generates torque, which is proportional to the viscosity of the sample.<br><br>
Important factors affecting viscosity measurement include:<br>
<b>Spindle type</b> – Different spindles are used depending on sample viscosity.<br>
<b>Rotational speed</b> – Determines the shear rate applied to the sample.<br>
<b>Temperature</b> – Viscosity decreases as temperature increases.<br><br>
Many pharmaceutical semisolids exhibit non-Newtonian flow behaviour, meaning their viscosity changes with shear rate.<br>
Examples include:<br>
•	Creams<br>
•	Gels<br>
•	Ointments<br>
